Anticonvulsants

Introduction

Seizures are sudden attacks of involuntary muscular contractions and relaxations which are electrically mediated from the CNS due to the change in normal firing of the neurons. Seizure can result due to high concentrations of the excitatory neurotransmitters (Glutamate and Aspartate) or low concentrations of inhibitory neurotransmitters (GABA).

Etiology of seizures

Ions involved are Na+ (influx causes presynaptic excitation) K+ (efflux causes postsynaptic inhibition), Cl- (influx causes postsynaptic inhibition) and Ca++ (influx causes postsynaptic excitation). Thus, treatment strategy is targeted at blocking actions of glutamate NT (also including blockage of Na+ channels), enhancing actions of GABA (which causes influx of Cl-), blocking of K+ channels (that inhibits efflux of K+ channels) and blockage of Ca++ (which inhibits influx of Ca++).

Drug targets

  1. Synthesis of GABA

    From its precursor, Glutamate, by Glutamate Decarboxylase (GAD). Inhibited by Pregabalin and Valproate.

  2. Effect of GABA

    GABA enhances Cl- channel opening, and influx of Cl- causes hyperpolarization (inhibitory effect). This is targeted by three types of drugs: Benzodiazepines, Barbiturates, and GABA analogs, and they all have an agonist action on the GABA-A receptor.

  3. Uptake of GABA

    By the GABA transporter; which is inhibited by Tiagabine.

  4. GABA metabolism

    To its metabolites by GABA transaminase (GABA-T) which is inhibited by valproate and vigabatrin.

  5. Effect of Glutamate

    Binding to the NMDA receptor causes excitation of postsynaptic neurons, antagonized by Lamotrigene and Felbamate.

  6. Role of Sodium

    Entry of Na+ causes depolarization of presynaptic neurons which causes release of excitatory NT like glutamate. Na+ channel is blocked by phenytoin and carbamazepine, valproate, topiramate, and zonisamide.

  7. Role of Calcium

    Entry of Ca++ in the postsynaptic neuron cause propagation of an action potential. Ethosuximide and valproate increase the threshold of Ca++ spikes thus reducing the Ca++ currents.

Classification

Classification depends on;

Mechanism of action

Effect on GABA

Valproate, Zonisamide, Topiramate, Benzodiazepines, Barbiturates, Vigabatrin, Tiagabine, Pregabalin, Levetiracetem, Gabapentin

Effect on Na+

Phenytoin, Carbamazepine, Lamotrigine, Oxcarbazepine, Felbamate, Topiramate, Valproate, Zonisamide

Effect on Ca++

Valproate, Zonisamide, Pregabalin, Levetiracetam, Gabapentin, Ethosuximide

Chemical classification

  • Carboxamides – carbamazepine
  • Hydantoins – phenytoin
  • Barbiturates – phenobarbital
  • Succinimides – ethosuximide
  • Valproates – sodium valproate
  • Benzodiazepines – clonazepam, clorazepate, diazepam, lorazepam, nitrazepam
  • GABA analogues – gabapentin and tiagabine
  • Heterogenic compounds – lamotrigine, levetiracetam, pregabalin, topiramate, vigabatrin

Effect on CYP450 enzymes

Inducers

Phenytoin, Carbamazepine, Phenobarbital These increase clearance, and thus decrease the effect of OCOs, warfarin, and theophylline.

Inhibitors

Valproate These decrease clearance, and thus increase the effect of the affected drugs

Drug properties

CBMZ

  • Metabolite is neurotoxic and has higher anticonvulsant properties.
  • Causes agranulocytosis.
  • DOC for trigeminal neuralgia.
  • Backup drug for bipolar disorders (after lithium).

Phenytoin

  • Has saturable metabolic system, and gets saturated when drug concentrations are high, causing toxicity.
  • Has variable absorption
  • Has a very high protein binding capacity
  • Has dose dependent elimination curve (shifts from 1st order to zero order at high doses).
  • Never used in absence seizures.
  • Toxicity is best remembered by HOTMALIKA: Hyperplasia of gingivae, Osteomalacia, Teratogenic, Megaloblastic anemia, Ataxia,
  • Lymphadenopathy, Insulin release inhibition, Vitamin K deficiency, Arrhythmias.

Gabapentin

In combination with CBMZ; used for treatment of shooting and stabbing neuropathic pain which responds poorly to analgesics.

Topiramate

Is associated with myopia and closed angle glaucoma

Phenobarbital

  • More sedative
  • Withdraw symptoms precipitate status epilepticus

Therapeutic app.

  1. General tonic clonic (Grand Mal) and partial seizures: Valproate, Lamotrigene, CBMZ, phenytoin, topiramate, vigabatrin, phenobarbital, gabapentin, tiagabine
  2. Petit-Mal (Absence seizures): Ethosuximide, Valproate
  3. Status epilepticus: Lorazepam, Diazepam, Phenytoin, Propofol, Thiopental
